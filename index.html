Keep Us Connected

The Fight for Universal Internet

The Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP) plays a crucial role in ensuring high-speed internet access for low-income Americans, crucial for education and healthcare. Yet, this essential program faces jeopardy from political gridlock, with potential funding cuts threatening to sever millions from these vital services. Discover the importance and impact of the ACP here.

The possible discontinuation of the ACP poses a severe threat to social equity and democratic participation, impacting more than just internet access—it also influences educational achievements and civic engagement. This project uncovers some of the extensive implications of recognizing digital access as a fundamental right, advocating for the continuation of the ACP to foster inclusive community involvement.

The Necessity of Internet Access

In the modern digital era, internet access is more than a luxury; it is a fundamental human right crucial for both individual and community progression. This belief is reinforced by Article 19 of the the UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights, which supports the freedom to seek, receive, and impart information across all media.

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the essential role of digital connectivity, with remote learning, telecommuting, and telemedicine becoming necessities. These shifts have exposed significant access disparities, notably affecting economically disadvantaged groups, those with disabilities, non-native language speakers, and remote communities, deepening existing societal divides.

Recognizing the critical role of internet connectivity, it is vital that policymakers and community leaders treat it not just as a service but as an essential right, akin to water and electricity, ensuring no one is left behind in our increasingly digital world.

IMPACT ON EMPLOYMENT AND POVERTY RATES →

Impact on Employment and Poverty Rates

Internet access is crucial in today's digital economy, serving as a key driver for educational and economic opportunities. Studies consistently show that inadequate internet access correlates with higher poverty and unemployment rates, especially in areas where digital skills are essential for employment. Using data from the Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS), I created visualizations that highlight the relationship between lack of internet access, poverty rates, and unemployment across the USA.

1. No Internet by Poverty Level

The line graph shows the relationship between poverty levels and households without internet access. Higher poverty levels are linked to increased lack of internet connectivity, highlighting the digital divide faced by economically disadvantaged communities.

2. Broadband Availability by Poverty Level

The accompanying bar graph illustrates the inverse relationship between economic status and broadband access, emphasizing the struggles faced by economically disadvantaged communities in securing reliable internet.

3. Broadband Availability by Unemployment Rate

The boxplot shows that higher unemployment rates often coincide with sporadic and inadequate broadband access, suggesting a cyclical relationship where poor internet connectivity can both contribute to and result from higher unemployment rates.

Act Now: What You Can Do →

Act Now: What You Can Do

The future of the Affordable Connectivity Program hangs in the balance. Your voice can influence its continuation. Here are several ways to advocate for this essential service:

  1. Sign Petitions: Look for and sign petitions that advocate for the continuation and adequate funding of the ACP. These petitions help show widespread support and can influence policymakers.
  2. Contact Your Legislators and the White House: It's crucial that your representatives understand the importance of the ACP. Reach out, and if you've already done so, follow up to emphasize your concerns.
  3. Join Community Advocacy Groups: Engage with local and national organizations that work towards digital equity. These groups often organize events, rallies, and meetings that can help further the cause and put additional pressure on legislators.
  4. Educate Others: Spread the word about the importance of affordable internet access. The more people are aware of what is at stake, the stronger the public call for action will be.
